## The search landscape just shifted under your feet

If you run a local business in Utah, your SEO strategy probably looks something like this: optimize your website, build some backlinks, manage your Google Business Profile, and hope you show up on page one.

That worked in 2023. It's increasingly insufficient in 2026.

Here's why: **60% of Google searches now end with zero clicks.** The user gets their answer from an AI-generated summary at the top of the page and never scrolls down to the traditional blue links. Google calls these "AI Overviews," and they now appear on more than half of all search result pages.

But it goes further than Google. Your customers are increasingly asking **ChatGPT, Perplexity, and other AI assistants** for recommendations directly. "Who's the best HVAC company in Draper?" "Find me a dentist near Sandy." "What real estate agent should I use in Herriman?"

If your business doesn't appear in those AI-generated answers, you're invisible to a growing share of your market.

## So what is GEO?

**Generative Engine Optimization (GEO)** is the practice of optimizing your business to appear in AI-powered search results — not just traditional Google rankings.

Think of it this way:

- **Traditional SEO** optimizes for Google's ranking algorithm. The goal is to appear on page 1 of search results.
- **GEO** optimizes for the large language models (LLMs) that power ChatGPT, Perplexity, Google AI Overviews, and voice assistants. The goal is to get **cited or recommended** in AI-generated answers.

These are related but different disciplines. A business can rank well on Google and still be completely absent from AI search results. The signals that LLMs use to decide which businesses to recommend are different from the signals Google uses to rank websites.

## What GEO actually involves

GEO isn't one tactic — it's a combination of six disciplines:

### 1. Entity Optimization

AI models need to "know" your business exists. This means having structured data (schema markup) on your website, a presence in Google's knowledge graph, and consistent business information (name, address, phone) across the web. If the AI doesn't have a clear, authoritative record of your business, it can't recommend you.

### 2. AI Citation Building

LLMs generate recommendations based on what they've learned from the web. Getting your business mentioned on authoritative sources — industry directories, local publications, news sites, high-trust review platforms — increases the likelihood that an AI will surface your business when asked for a recommendation.

### 3. Structured Content

AI systems prefer content they can easily parse and cite. This means writing in clear, direct formats: FAQ sections with schema markup, direct answers to common questions, statistics with sources, and well-organized information hierarchies. If your content is buried in marketing fluff, AI will skip over it.

### 4. Review Signal Management

AI assistants weight reviews heavily when making recommendations. Volume matters, but so does recency, quality, and platform diversity. A business with 200 reviews averaging 4.8 stars across Google, Yelp, and industry-specific platforms is far more likely to be recommended than one with 50 reviews only on Google.

### 5. Digital Authority & PR

Brand mentions across the web feed into AI models' understanding of who is trustworthy and relevant. Strategic PR, guest contributions, podcast appearances, and industry citations build the authority signals that LLMs use to decide which businesses deserve a recommendation.

### 6. AI Search Monitoring

You can't improve what you don't measure. GEO requires tracking whether your business appears when people ask ChatGPT, Perplexity, and Google AI Overviews for recommendations in your service area. This is a new measurement discipline that most agencies don't even know exists.
